# Marketing Brief — The Weekly Thing

This file is the persistent context for the home-page pull-quote generator (`pipeline/content/refresh_marketing_copy.py`, which writes `apps/site/_data/voiceSamples.json`). It's read at the start of every refresh run and rewritten at the end so observations accumulate over time. Git is the version history. It is **not** the canonical voice doc — that's [`docs/voice-and-style.md`](../../docs/voice-and-style.md); the Voice / What makes it unique / What to avoid sections below should track it.

Jamie can hand-edit this file at any time. Whatever is here on the next run becomes ground truth.

## Voice

- First-person, observational, dry, curious. Not salesy.
- Short sentences mixed with longer reflective ones. Comfortable with asides.
- Writes as a reader and a practitioner, not a pundit. Avoids "thought leader" framing.
- Candid about opinions but rarely hot-takes. Notices things other people skim past.
- Comfortable zooming from a Wikipedia rabbit hole to a technology trend to a personal anecdote in the same issue.

## Recurring themes

_Updated by the creative team each run. Keep concrete and evidence-based._

- Open web and IndieWeb: defends RSS, hyperlinks, ActivityPub, and personal blogs against platform enclosure in nearly every issue.
- Personal health as data: tracks own caffeine, sleep, blood pressure, and glucose with specific numbers and honest null results.
- Agentic AI from practitioner POV: moves from reading to building agents, documenting real workflow including failures.
- Privacy as structural problem: frames surveillance capitalism and encryption backdoors as requiring legislation, not just personal workarounds.
- Career-long learning ethic: returns repeatedly to the idea that a tech career requires continuous reinvention and deliberate learning.
- Minnesota and family as anchor: local places, seasonal details, and family milestones ground every issue in a specific lived life.

## What makes it unique

_Observable attributes from the corpus — things that are actually true of this newsletter, not aspirational claims._

- Published continuously since May 13, 2017. Three platforms, one voice.
- No algorithm deciding what you see. One person reading widely, linking with context.
- Commentary, not aggregation: each link has a reason it's in the issue.
- Range is the feature, not a bug: technology, the indie web, personal reflection, hobbies, family, local Minnesota things.
- Privacy-preserving by design: no click tracking, no pixels, no data harvesting.
- Free for everyone, always. Membership is pure charity — 100% to a nonprofit.

## What to avoid

Strict guardrails. These override everything else.

- No superlatives ("best", "amazing", "unmatched", "premier").
- No empty marketing phrases ("cutting-edge", "curated with care", "carefully crafted", "hand-picked").
- No "thought leader" / "in the trenches" / "on the bleeding edge" language.
- No invented statistics. Numbers come from `archiveStats.js` or directly from sampled issues.
- No claims about reader benefits that aren't demonstrable from the archive.
- No second-person imperatives that sound like ad copy ("Level up your reading!"). Prefer declarative framing.
- **No "one person" / "just one person" / "one curious person" framing anywhere** — not in taglines, headlines, value-prop paragraphs, CTAs, or theme descriptions. Jamie has said he doesn't like it (feels small/marketing-ish). Use a different angle: commentary over algorithm, sustained for 8+ years, specific range of topics, human editorial judgment, reader-grounded language from the survey, etc.
- No emoji in generated copy unless an issue subject contains one and we're quoting it.
- Prefer specific over general: "47 links about AI agents in the last year" beats "stay on top of AI."

## Open observations

_Running notes from the creative team between runs. Free-form. Used to stay coherent across refreshes._

- The April 2024–April 2026 sample crosses a clear inflection point on AI: earlier issues treat it as one topic among many; later issues document Jamie's direct practice as a builder of agents, with real workflow detail including failures and costs.
- The journal section (family photos, escape rooms, North Shore hikes, Minnesota weather) functions as social proof that the newsletter is a whole-person artifact, not just a tech digest. Worth preserving when copy drifts too technical.
- Reader language around "trust" and "coffee with someone curious" maps cleanly onto what the issues actually do: each link gets a short personal rationale. That editorial move — commentary, not aggregation — is the sharpest differentiator and should stay load-bearing in the value prop.
- Privacy and open-web threads are the most consistent throughlines across the whole sample. They read as genuine convictions, not topical choices, and they pair naturally with the no-tracking promise on the site itself.
- Voice markers to keep in mind: sincere use of bold and exclamation points, open acknowledgement of reversals and failed experiments, specific numbers even when approximate, and links that reference each other inside a single issue.
