### [Company, team, self. | Irrational Exuberance](https://lethain.com/company-team-self/)

Larson reflecting that you must thing about the humans in mix, and what brings them energy, as you think about the priority of the work that you tackle.

> Back to the main topic: correctness and humans mix in complex ways. The most important lesson I’ve learned as I’ve become a better manager is that there is almost always a correct answer, but applying that answer to your specific situation will always be nuanced and messy. Further, the correct answer is almost always different if you’re taking a short-term or long-term perspective. The focus on what provides energy is a good one.

### [The Need to Read](http://paulgraham.com/read.html)

Reading makes you a better writer. Writing is thinking.

> But if you need to solve a complicated, ill-defined problem, it will almost always help to write about it. Which in turn means that someone who's not good at writing will almost always be at a disadvantage in solving such problems.

Reading will make you a better thinker, if augmented with writing.

This is one of the reasons I blog and write the Weekly Thing.

### [The 7 Habits of Highly Overrated People - DaedTech](https://daedtech.com/the-7-habits-of-highly-overrated-people/)

The title of this article caught my eye. Dietrich combines an array of experiences and highlights the patterns that he has seen "highly overrated" people exhibit.

> 1. "Overcommunicate"
> 2. Be Bossy and Critical
> 3. Shamelessly Self Promote
> 4. Distract with Arguments about Minutiae
> 5. Time It So You Look Good (Or Everyone Else Looks Bad)
> 6. Plan Excuses Ahead of Time
> 7. Take Credit in Non-Disprovable Ways

Many of these things would be easy to spot, but a good read particularly for managers to keep an eye out for some practices that are meant to mislead and obfuscate.

### [Opinion | Americans are spending more time alone. Let's reverse that. - The Washington Post](https://www.washingtonpost.com/opinions/2022/11/23/americans-alone-thanksgiving-friends/)

These numbers continue to trend down, and none of this seems like it can be good for us.

> By 2019, the average American was spending only four hours per week with friends (a sharp, 37 percent decline from five years before). Social media, political polarization and new technologies all played a role in the drop. (It is notable that market penetration for smartphones crossed 50 percent in 2014.)

I was a little shocked to see that none of this now unused time is going to those we love either.

> On average, Americans did not transfer that lost time to spouses, partners or children. Instead, they chose to be alone.

We humans have a profound need to connect with others, which I totally think is a fact. We are also so anxious and avoidant of that need that it seems that little glowing piece of glass can overrid that fundamental need entirely. Related reading, [Bowling Alone](http://bowlingalone.com).

### [The promise of social media - without bullshit](https://withoutbullshit.com/blog/the-promise-of-social-media)

Bernoff assesses the current state of social and asks the important question:

> I wonder if it is even possible to have a mode of social media interaction that…

He enumerates a few objectives, but the main point is can you really have a big social media platform that works.

**I think the answer to this is no.**

I had a few breakfast debates on this with [Lee Zukor](https://leezukor.com) years ago and it helped form my opinion that the problem with social media isn't the specific implmentation of Facebook or Twitter, but is instead the thing itself. You can’t optimize and improve it, because the core thing is no good. It’s like smoking cigarettes. There is no version of it that isn't bad for you.

### [Protect Me From What I Want • ongoing by Tim Bray](https://www.tbray.org/ongoing/When/202x/2022/11/28/On-Algorithms)

Bray offers an optimisic perspective on using algorithms to display content to people, however with a very huge caveat. I have added the bold.

> An algorithm is not intrinsically bad. **As long as we understand that it represents the interests of whoever paid to have it constructed.** I think an algorithm with human values that simply wanted to enrich experience is perfectly possible.
>
> I haven't seen one, probably because nobody has ever had a financial incentive to construct it.

I shared in [Weekly Thing #152](https://weekly.thingelstad.com/archive/weekly-thing-152-juneteenth-uncomfortable/), highlighting that any algorithm must have a goal.

> Anytime that you are looking at content in something other than time order, ask yourself what the goal of the system behind it is.

Connect Bray's point and mine, and you get the problem. Bray highlights good algorithms that help us filter through noise, and suggests a "Algorithms for the people by the people" kind of idea. It would be cool to see such a thing, but most place that isn't possible. His idea of using Mastadon as a place for experimentation is a good one. Bray himself would hate my additional suggestion (he's a strong critic of all things crypto), of using data held in public blockchains which require full open access to both the data and the function.

### [Opinion: FTX failed because it veered from decentralized finance - MarketWatch](https://www.marketwatch.com/story/long-live-crypto-ftxs-failure-doesnt-diminish-the-worthy-purpose-of-decentralized-finance-11669140719)

"Not your keys, not your crypto." Many people I know in crypto didn’t fall for the FTX fraud because of a close aherence to that principle — they keep their crypto on their own and self-custody. FTX of course offered incredible interest rates and yield on assets you left with them, which was the other reason many people weren't affected — it was simply too good to be true.

### [Seeing through hardware counters: a journey to threefold performance increase | Netflix TechBlog](https://netflixtechblog.com/seeing-through-hardware-counters-a-journey-to-threefold-performance-increase-2721924a2822)

You don't often get to see performance debugging down at the level of the JVM, but this is a pretty interesting read. The problem is framed up:

> Although we confirmed fairly equal traffic distribution between nodes, CPU and latency metrics surprisingly demonstrated a very different, bimodal distribution pattern. There is a "lower band" of nodes exhibiting much lower CPU and latency with hardly any variation; and there is an "upper band" of nodes with significantly higher CPU/latency and wide variation. We noticed only ~12% of the nodes fall into the lower band, a figure that was suspiciously consistent over time. In both bands, performance characteristics remain consistent for the entire uptime of the JVM on the node, i.e. nodes never jumped the bands. This was our starting point for troubleshooting.

Ultimately comes down to how the cache is laid out in physical memory!

Legitimacy as an asset that is built and withdrawn from is something that I think deserves more attention. Legitimacy also comes into play with software. What makes a system legitimate? These are interesting human system questions.
