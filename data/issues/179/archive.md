## Must Read

### [The Director Series - Board Topics of Today - Insights from Professional Board Member Sven Wehrwein - YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=w61Ck7akqCs&t=378s)

This [Club E](https://www.clube.com) interview with [Sven Wehrwein](https://www.linkedin.com/in/sven-wehrwein-b86aa88/) provides some great insight on how he approaches his board work. Sven is on the [SPS Commerce](https://www.spscommerce.com) board and I have had the great privilege of working with him and getting to know him a bit. Sven has been on **9 public boards**, **5 private boards**. He has over **70 years on public boards**. He's an expert.

Some great highlights Sven shared:

1. He kept track for a year to see how much time it takes to be on a board. He averaged it out to **225 hours** a year per board.
2. It is interesting to hear the dialogue between Sven and [Jim Zuehlke](https://www.linkedin.com/in/jim-zuehlke/), [Rick Brimacomb](https://www.linkedin.com/in/rickbrimacomb/) sharing some of the board committee structures they have experienced.
2. Approach board work with **Nose-in, Fingers-out (NIFO)** strategy. Get your nose into things, but keep your fingers out. **Socratic Method**: Ask questions to drive to better understanding. Don't give orders. **Effectiveness**: Are you helping move the company forward?
3. Sven has a mantra, **No Surprises.** Audit committees cannot have surprises. He knows this is effective when he sees it affecting the things that he is brought in on, and when.
4. General leadership comment "All good leaders have a message that will **resonant**, are **clear**, and **repeat**."

They also shared that there will be a part two of this conversation!

### [Senior Engineers Build Consensus : hyperbo.la](https://hyperbo.la/w/nemawashi/)

The consensus building aspect that Senior Engineers play in organizations is frequently overlooked by people as they take these roles.

> Nemawashi is a tool for helping the organization do the right thing by default. The goal of a senior engineer should be to identify what those right things are and set the organization up for success by removing roadblocks.

Often times Senior engineers may take the approach of "let me show you how it’s done", and without building consensus with the very smart and talented engineers around you, the results often don't realize.

I also found this [Nemawashi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nemawashi) concept interesting. I've seen and done the practice, but didn’t know there was a name.

## Reply All

Here are some replies from [Weekly Thing #178 / Reverse Meetings, OODA Loop, Hockey Goalies](https://weekly.thingelstad.com/archive/weekly-thing-178-reverse-meetings-ooda-loop/).

[Paul Birkbeck](https://www.linkedin.com/in/paulbirkbeck/) enjoyed the article on Hockey Goalies and sent this great reply that I want to share his whole reply with all of you! 🏒

I loved the inclusion of the goalie article. I have something for you that will bend your head a little… it’s super obvious when you see it, but nothing you’ve likely ever considered… it’s called the **Shooter’s Illusion**.

You’re a player, you are a right handed shot meaning that your left hand is on the upper part of the stick and right hand down lower controlling the stick.  You’re coming in on the right side and you are pretty close to the goalie.  He’s already down in the butterfly stance and you get pretty excited because of all the room he’s left you to shoot blocker side.  Sure goal in your mind!

![](https://files.thingelstad.com/weekly-thing/2021/20210327-reply-all-hockey-1.jpg)

You take aim for pretty much anywhere on the blocker side… but miraculous save by the goalie.  Or was it?  Now let’s put a camera on the puck… which is 5’6” lower than your eyes and about 3’ further in front of you:

![](https://files.thingelstad.com/weekly-thing/2021/20210327-reply-all-hockey-2.jpg)

In reality, you had nothing to shoot at.  Goalie’s will change the angle of the gloves, put them further out in front and actually tilt them downward.  To a player, it looks like the glove is closed, or out of position, but in actuality, because the puck is coming on an upward angle, the glove is more open to the puck but appears more closed from the player perspective.  Next time you are watching a hockey game, notice that oftentimes when a player is in close and shooting, he will change the angle of the shot, oftentimes by bringing the puck in closer, or take a quick ½ step in a different direction before shooting.  The goalie has set up for where the puck is, so by changing the angle of the shot, they increase their chances of scoring greatly.

![](https://files.thingelstad.com/weekly-thing/2021/20210327-reply-all-hockey-3.jpg)

The article shows a few more angles but it’s an interesting optical illusion in hockey that most people don’t know about.
