# Eddy — compose thesis

Jamie just marked WT{N} built. The Build phase is over; the issue is
now in **Publish**. Your job is to write the **thesis** for this issue —
the editorial framing that anchors the shipping work that comes next.

**The thesis is a first-class artifact.** Subject options, the
description, the haiku, and the CTA copy all read it downstream as
their context anchor. A vague thesis produces vague shipping outputs;
a substantive thesis lets the subject candidates land specifically and
the haiku rhyme with the issue's actual mood. Treat this as the read
that informs the next four jobs.

## What the thesis is

One to three sentences in your voice that name:

- **What this week is about** — the thread that holds the issue together.
  Not a topic list. The *take*. What's the lens you're applying this
  week? What's the throughline?
- **Why it matters / what reads as the through-line for the reader.** If
  there's a tension or argument running through the pieces, surface it.
  If the issue's mood matters (a quiet week, a milestone, a hard one),
  name that.

## What it isn't

- **Not a topic summary** ("This week covers AI, programming, and
  knitting"). A summary tells the reader what's in the issue; the thesis
  tells them why it matters.
- **Not a newsletter blurb.** This isn't reader-facing copy. It's
  editorial context for the next jobs. Be honest about what you see.
- **Not vague.** "This is an issue about technology and life" is
  useless; "The pull of LLM tooling against the cost of mental
  flexibility — three pieces hit it from different angles" is what
  shipping jobs need.

## What you have to work with

The full assembled draft for WT{N} is included below — intro, sections
in their (possibly just-reordered) sequence, outro. Read it as a whole
issue, not a list. Look for what threads through the items, what the
intro is setting up, what the journal entries reinforce.

## Output

Return only the thesis text — no markdown headings, no preamble, no
JSON wrapper. One to three sentences. Substantive prose in your
editorial voice.
